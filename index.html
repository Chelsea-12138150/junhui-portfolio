02 — About Me

The why behind the work.

I started in Economics and Finance, where I learned to think in systems, structure problems, and work with data. But somewhere between the spreadsheets and the regression tables, a different question started pulling at me — not how decisions are made, but why people make them.

That curiosity led me to Behavioural Science. I'm now pursuing my master's at the National University of Singapore, studying how human behaviour, context, and evidence can shape better interventions — and better products.

Across research, product experiments, and AI tools, I care most about turning insight into something useful. Whether I'm cleaning a dataset, testing a workflow, or decoding user behaviour, I'm after the same thing: understanding people well enough to design with both empathy and clarity.

# dissertation.do — caregiver behaviour profiles
import nhats as panel
from stata import lca, mixed_effects

toolkit = [
'STATA', 'Python', 'Excel', 'panel data',
'latent class analysis', 'mixed-effects models'
]

def research(caregivers):
    baseline = caregivers.identify('1,250 baseline caregivers')
    long_panel = panel.harmonize('14 NHATS waves')
    classes = lca.fit(baseline, indicators=[
        'sleep disorder', 'walking', 'vigorous activity'
    ])
    trajectories = mixed_effects.estimate(
        outcomes=['depression', 'cognition'],
        group_by=classes
    )
    return trajectories # behaviour profiles linked to health over time
— FIG. 1 · CAREGIVER BEHAVIOURAL PROFILES
→ 2 behavioural profiles across sleep and activity
— MODEL OUTPUT
Class 1 46.2% less healthy
Class 2 53.8% health-engaged
Linked class membership to depression and cognition across 14 waves.
— Margin Note

Built a longitudinal NHATS panel from 14 waves; used latent class analysis to identify two caregiver behavioural profiles, then linked class membership through mixed-effects models.

☕ Field Notes

Every good idea I have probably started somewhere between a coffee cup, a laptop, and a half-finished to-do list.

Cafés have become my unofficial thinking rooms. I go there to read papers, rewrite slides, debug messy ideas, and turn scattered thoughts into something structured. Some places are good for deep work. Some are better for people-watching. Some are where I go when my brain needs a reset before it can start again.

For me, cafés are not escapes from work. They are small stations of attention. I go there to read, write, think, reset, and return to difficult problems with a clearer mind. It is a simple habit, but it carries a larger belief: the right environment can make discipline feel almost gentle.

"A good café does not just serve coffee. It lends me a better version of my mind."

The Goose Detective

🕵️ Social Deduction

I have a soft spot for games that reward a sharp brain and a calm face.

Goose Goose Duck is chaotic on the surface, but underneath the noise there is structure. A strange vote, a late accusation, a sentence that feels too polished, a player who suddenly becomes quiet. I like catching those small signals before they become obvious to everyone else.

The fun part is not simply winning. It is building a theory with limited information, testing it in real time, and deciding how much to reveal so others will follow your logic without feeling pushed.

It is playful, but the skill is real: reading the room, thinking several steps ahead, and making decisions before all the evidence is available.

"The smartest move is not always to speak first. Sometimes it is to notice first."
